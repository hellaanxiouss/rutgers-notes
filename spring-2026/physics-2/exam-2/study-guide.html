Mixed Series-Parallel Network (All Equal C) Level II
Fig. 24-23 — Four capacitors, all C
In Fig. 24-23, C₁ = C₂ = C₃ = C₄ = C.
(a) Determine the equivalent capacitance between points a and b.
(b) Find the charge on each capacitor and the potential difference across each, in terms of V.
Circuit Layout: C₁ and C₂ are in series forming the top branch. C₃ is alone in a middle branch. C₄ is alone in the bottom branch. All three branches connect in parallel between points a and b.

Parallel: Both ends of each component connect to the same two nodes → same voltage across all.
Series: Components chained end-to-end with nothing branching off → same current (same charge for caps) through all.

In Fig. 24-23: C₁ and C₂ share only an internal node (no other branch attached there) → they're in series. The resulting C₁₂ pair, plus C₃, plus C₄ all connect directly between a and b → they're in parallel.

  1. 1
    C₁ and C₂ in series: 1/C₁₂ = 1/C + 1/C = 2/C → C₁₂ = C/2
  2. 2
    Three branches in parallel: C_eq = C₁₂ + C₃ + C₄ = C/2 + C + C = 5C/2
  3. 3
    All parallel branches have the same voltage V.
    Q₃ = C·V, Q₄ = C·V
  4. 4
    Series branch carries charge Q = C₁₂·V = (C/2)·V
    Q₁ = Q₂ = CV/2 (same charge for caps in series)
  5. 5
    Voltages: V₁ = Q₁/C = V/2, V₂ = V/2 ✓ (check: V₁+V₂ = V ✓)
    V₃ = V, V₄ = V
✅ C_eq = 5C/2  |  Q₁=Q₂=CV/2  |  Q₃=Q₄=CV

Mixed Network — Numerical Values Level II
Same circuit as P29 but with real numbers. Given Q₂ = 12.4 μC.
C₁ = C₂ = C₃ = 16.0 μF, C₄ = 28.5 μF. Charge on C₂: Q₂ = 12.4 μC.
Find (a) charge on C₁, C₃, C₄   (b) voltage V across combination   (c) total energy stored.
Key Insight: C₁ and C₂ are in series → they carry the same charge. So Q₁ = Q₂ = 12.4 μC immediately. Then work backwards to find V, and use V to find Q₃ and Q₄.
  1. 1
    C₁ ∥ C₂ are in series → Q₁ = Q₂ = 12.4 μC
  2. 2
    Voltage across series branch: V = Q₁/C₁ + Q₂/C₂ = 12.4/16 + 12.4/16 = 0.775 + 0.775 = 1.55 V
  3. 3
    All parallel branches share V = 1.55 V:
    Q₃ = 16.0×10⁻⁶ × 1.55 = 24.8 μC
    Q₄ = 28.5×10⁻⁶ × 1.55 = 44.2 μC
  4. 4
    Total charge: Q_total = Q_series + Q₃ + Q₄ = 12.4 + 24.8 + 44.2 = 81.4 μC
    C_eq = Q_total/V = 81.4μC / 1.55V = 52.5 μF
  5. 5
    Energy: U = ½·C_eq·V² = ½ × 52.5×10⁻⁶ × (1.55)² = 63.1 μJ

Capacitance Bridge (Null Method) Level II
Fig. 24-26 — Find unknown Cₓ from null condition
A voltage V₀ is applied to the capacitance bridge. Variable capacitor C₁ is adjusted until V_ab = 0 (voltmeter reads zero). Find the unknown capacitance Cₓ.

Given: C₁ = 8.9 μF, C₂ = 18.0 μF, C₃ = 4.8 μF
Bridge Null Condition: When V_ab = 0, no current through voltmeter. The voltage divides proportionally on each side. This is exactly like a Wheatstone resistor bridge — just swap R → 1/C.
  1. 1
    Two voltage divider arms: Left (C₁ and Cₓ in series), Right (C₂ and C₃ in series)
  2. 2
    Null condition: voltage ratio same on both sides → Cₓ/C₁ = C₃/C₂
    (In capacitor dividers, smaller C gets larger share of voltage, so the ratios invert relative to resistors)
  3. 3
    Cₓ = C₁ · C₃ / C₂ = 8.9 × 4.8 / 18.0 = 42.72 / 18.0 = 2.37 μF

Fig. 26-49 — 45V and 75V batteries, three resistors
Circuit in Fig. 26-49: 45V battery with 47Ω (left), 75V battery with 18Ω (right), shared 34Ω in middle.
(a) Potential difference between points a and d. (b) Terminal voltage of each battery.
Kirchhoff Procedure: (1) Label currents with arrows. (2) Write node equation. (3) Write one KVL loop per unknown. (4) Solve the system. (5) Trace path for V_ad.

    Assign: I₁ upward in left branch, I₂ upward in right branch, I₃ downward in middle branch.
    Node rule at top: I₁ + I₂ = I₃
  2. 2
    Loop 1 (left + middle, clockwise): +45 − 47·I₁ − 34·I₃ = 0
    Substitute I₃ = I₁+I₂: 45 = 81·I₁ + 34·I₂
  3. 3
    Loop 2 (right + middle, counterclockwise): +75 − 18·I₂ − 34·I₃ = 0

    Solve the 2×2 system → I₁ ≈ 0.315 A, I₂ ≈ 1.237 A, I₃ ≈ 1.553 A
  5. 5
    For V_ad: trace path a→b→c→d summing +/− for each battery and resistor passed.
    V_ad = +45 − 47·I₁ (check sign with your current directions)

    Both positive → assumed directions were correct. I₃ = 0.166 + 0.645 = 0.811 A through middle R.

Compass Deflection Near a Wire Level II
Vector addition of B_wire and Earth's B field
A horizontal compass is placed 18 cm due south from a straight vertical wire carrying 43 A downward.
Earth's horizontal field = 0.45 × 10⁻⁴ T, declination = 0°.
In what direction does the compass needle point?
Method: Find B_wire (magnitude + direction), then add vectorially to B_Earth pointing North. The resultant gives the compass direction.
  1. 1
    Current flows downward. Point thumb DOWN. Fingers at the south point (below the wire) curl toward the WEST. So B_wire points WEST at the compass location.
  2. 2
    |B_wire| = μ₀I/(2πr) = (4π×10⁻⁷ × 43) / (2π × 0.18)
    = (4×10⁻⁷ × 43) / (2 × 0.18) = 4.78 × 10⁻⁵ T West
  3. 3
    B_Earth = 4.5 × 10⁻⁵ T North
    B_wire = 4.78 × 10⁻⁵ T West
  4. 4
    Angle from North: θ = arctan(B_wire/B_Earth) = arctan(4.78/4.5) = arctan(1.062) = 46.7° west of north

Solenoid Design — Find Required Current Level I

A 40.0-cm-long solenoid (diameter 1.35 cm) with 765 turns must produce B = 0.385 mT at center.
How much current should flow?

  2. 2

ε = −N · dΦ_B/dt

Motional EMF
ε = BLv

Lenz's Law
Induced current OPPOSES the
CHANGE in flux that caused it.
Force on Moving Rod
F = BIL = B²L²v/R

r = mv/(qB)

Prove KE ∝ r² and L = qBr² Level II
Algebraic proof — circular motion in B field
For a particle (mass m, charge q) in circular path radius r in field B:
(a) Show kinetic energy ∝ r²   (b) Show angular momentum L = qBr²
Starting point for both parts: The circular motion condition gives qvB = mv²/r → solve for v = qBr/m. Then substitute into KE and L formulas.
  1. 1
    Circular condition: qvB = mv²/r → v = qBr/m
  2. 2
    (a) KE = ½mv² = ½m·(qBr/m)² = q²B²r² / (2m)
    Since q, B, m are constants: KE ∝ r² ✓ QED
  3. 3
    (b) Angular momentum: L = mvr
    Substitute v = qBr/m: L = m·(qBr/m)·r = qBr² ✓ QED

Circular Path Geometry — Find x-crossing & time Level III
Fig. 27-47 — B in z-direction, v₀ at 30° from y-axis
Particle (+q, mass m) in B = B₀k̂. At t = 0, speed v₀, velocity in xy-plane at 30° from y-axis.
At t = tₐ it crosses x-axis at x = a.
Find: (a) a   (b) tₐ   in terms of q, m, v₀, B₀.
This is Level III — the hardest problem assigned. B acts in z, so force is always in xy-plane. The particle moves in a circle in the xy-plane. Use geometry to find when it crosses the x-axis.
  1. 1
    B ∥ z → force F = qv×B is in xy-plane → pure circular motion in the xy-plane
  2. 2
    Radius: r = mv₀/(qB₀)
  3. 3
    At t = 0: particle at origin, velocity at 30° from +y toward +x.
    v_x = v₀·sin(30°) = v₀/2  ,  v_y = v₀·cos(30°) = v₀√3/2
  4. 4
    The center of the circular orbit is perpendicular to v₀, to the right of motion (use right-hand rule for +q in +z field). The center is at position (r·cos30°, −r·sin30°) = (r√3/2, −r/2) from the origin. Wait — let's think carefully: force on +q moving at 30° from +y in +z field. F = qv×B: v is in xy plane, B in +z, so F = q(v×z_hat) is rotated 90° clockwise from v. Center of circle is in F direction: rotated 90° clockwise from v₀ direction → center at (r·cos(30°−90°), ...). Work this out with: center = (r·sin30°, r·cos30°) ... use Fig 27-47 geometry.
  5. 5
    Particle crosses x-axis when y = 0. From circle equation: center at (r·sin30°, r·cos30°) = (r/2, r√3/2).
    Circle: (x − r/2)² + (y − r√3/2)² = r²
    Set y = 0: (x−r/2)² + 3r²/4 = r² → (x−r/2)² = r²/4 → x = r/2 ± r/2
    x = r (non-trivial crossing) → a = r = mv₀/(qB₀)
  6. 6
    Arc traveled: particle starts at angle 30° from x-axis of the circle... goes to point (r,0). The arc subtended = 120° = 2π/3 radians (from geometry).
    tₐ = (2π/3)·r/v₀ = (2π/3)·m/(qB₀) → tₐ = 2πm/(3qB₀)
